Abstract A tensegrity system is established when a set of discontinuous compressive components interacts with a set of continuous tensile components to dene a stable volume in space. The importance of Tensegrity in engineering applications stems from its ability to achieve maximum structural eciency by distinguishing between continuous tensile elements and discontinuous compressive elements. This structural separation ensures all members are under pure axial loading, virtually eliminating bending and shear stresses making the systems ideal for lightweight, modular, and deployable applications. Real-life scenarios include aerospace deployment mechanisms and large-span architectural structures In this project, we build a Tensegrity Table with a single tensional member. The primary objective of this project is to explore the principle of tensegrity and understand the workings of various modeling, simulation, analysis and workshop tools. The structure is designed and simulated in Autodesk Fusion 360. The manufacturing is then done in the Central Workshop using Wood, Nylon ropes and other materials with the help of hand-held and power-operated tools. This projects contributions are twofold.: 1. 0.1 Literature-Review 0.1.1 History The modern history of the concept is often tied to the iconoclastic American architect and inventor R. Buckminster Fuller, who coined the term in the 1960s (or as early as 1955). However, the rst physical structures embodying this principle were created earlier, notably by his student, the sculptor Kenneth Snelson, whose "X-Piece"[Figure 1] in 1948 demonstrated the visually striking system of "oating compression." The ideas origins may even predate this, with some scholars pointing to the early 20th-century work of Russian constructivist artist Karlis Iogansons. Fuller and Snelsons work, though marked by some controversy over attribution, popularized the idea, which has since been applied in architecture, engineering, and the scientic eld of biotensegrity[Figure 2] to understand structures in nature, such as the human cell and musculoskeletal system. 0.1.2 Topology The topology of a tensegrity structure refers to the pattern of connections between the elements, dening which nodes are linked by struts (compression members) and which are linked by cables (tension members), regardless of the structures specic geometry (nodal coordinates). This connectivity dictates the inherent self-stress modes (the internal force state required for stability) and the structures fundamental kinematic properties. Topology is often described using graph theory, where nodes are vertices and members (struts or cables) are edges. A key topological requirement for a classic tensegrity is discontinuous compression, meaning no two struts can share a node, ensuring that the rigid elements "oat" within the continuous network of tension elements. The classication of tensegrity structures often begins with their topology, such as the number of struts and their arrangement into basic unit cells like prisms and polyhedra. Munich Olympic Stadium (Germany): Designed by Frei Otto for the 1972 Summer Olympics, the stadium is a seminal example of cable-net and tensile archi- tecture, rather than a strict, pure tensegrity structure (where no two compression members touch). However, it beautifully embodies the core principles of tensegrity by using a system of discontinuous, rigid masts (compression) that support and sta- bilize a massive, continuous, lightweight cable-net canopy (tension). The structural integrity relies entirely on the precise balance of tension and compression, resulting in a dramatic, uid, and highly material-ecient roof structure. 2. Montreal Biosphere (Canada): Originally the US pavilion for Expo 67, this structure is a classic example of a geodesic dome, a concept popularized by R. Buck- minster Fuller (who also coined "tensegrity"). While a standard geodesic domes struts touch at the nodes (making it a traditional space frame), the principles of material eciency and the division of forces that inform tensegrity are highly evi- dent. Fullers work often transitioned betweenpure tensegrity and lightweight, load- sharing geodesic systems, and the Biosphere demonstrates the powerful application of lightweight, triangulated geometry to cover a vast space with minimal material. 3. This structure is a specic application of the tensegrity principle, where a portion of the structure appears to defy gravity by being suspended by tension elements (cables) while being stabilized by internal compression elements (the L-shaped struts) that do not directly touch each other or the top/bottom plates. Its stability relies entirely on the pre-stressed equilibrium achieved by the carefully balanced forces of tension and compression. 8
0.3 Stress-Analysis A Static Stress Analysis was performed on the Tensegrity Table model, incorporating gravitational eects and a uniform downward load of 500 N applied to the top hexagonal plate. The analysis successfully mapped the resulting stress, strain, and displacement across the structural members under these conditions. The displacement results indicate a maximum deection of 0.151 mm on the top plate, demonstrating a relatively small deformation under the applied load. The Von Mises stress analysis shows that the maximum stress of 14.742 MPa occurs primarily in one of the L-shaped compression struts. Critically, the strain analysis highlights the cable connecting the two L-shaped struts as the most critically strained component, registering the maximum strain value of 0.003. This result strongly suggests that this short, internal tension member connecting the oating compression elements is the structures weakest link, representing the likely point of failure under load.
